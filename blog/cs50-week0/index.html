CS50x - Week 0 Review

June 14, 2022

As I am still taking my first formative steps in my IT career, I find myself constantly curious on the different technologies and disciplines available. Consistently, there seems to be a single skill that is universally valued for it’s meaningful utility — programming.

I found the CS50x course in my pursuit for a litmus test for my affinity to the discipline, and as there seems to be a universal agreement on the quality of the course, I figured it perfectly suitable.

The CS50x team eloquently describes the course as the “introduction to the intellectual enterprises of computer science and the art of programming”. After finishing the Week 0 lecture, I’m both introduced to, and inclined to agree, that programming is an art.

The reductionist, yet passionate approach employed by David Malan in lecturing the topic was both highly intriguing and surprisingly useful.

As someone without a foundation in programming, learning to do so can seem like an intimidating peak that is infinitely as tall as it is wide. David shows that as complex as it may seem, all facets of the topics come from the fundamental requirement of people trying to solve problems and better develop tools to do this.

By boiling the complexity away to just the fundamentals, I find better perspective in viewing the intimidating peak for it’s achievable step-by-step foot holds, and less for it’s immense size and height.
Alex Honnold, viewing the 2,308m cliff face he would climb without a rope.

It strikes me that, programmers are the modern day class of people that all children (or at the very least, I did) aspire to be with all their imagination — inventors!

Listening on to historical snapshots of various computer science developments over time, (such as the application of binary due to the base-2 state constraints of hardware, or how ASCII was invented and agreed on out of desire to encompass human language within computers) it occurred to me that computer science was simply another application of creativity and problem solving.

I ended up spending a couple afternoon working on the activity within the Scratch programming interface — it was a great way to introduce beginners to the programming muscles of the brain, without first being contingent on language specifics. Looking forward to the coming weeks!
The starting screen to my side-scrolling game idea.
